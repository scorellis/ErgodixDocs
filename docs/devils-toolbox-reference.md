## Alliteration

**General Alliteration**: The most common form, where the same consonant
sound is repeated at the beginning of closely connected words.

> Example: "She sells seashells by the seashore."

**Consonance**: Repetition of consonant sounds, which can appear at the
beginning, middle, or end of words. When used at the beginning, it overlaps
with general alliteration.

> Example: "Mike likes his new bike."

**Assonance**: The repetition of vowel sounds within words that are close
together. While not strictly alliteration, it's often used in conjunction
with it.

> Example: "The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain."

**Symmetrical Alliteration**: A more complex form where a sequence of sounds
is mirrored around a central point.

> Example: "He who sees sees who he."

**Word Alliteration (or Full-Word Alliteration)**: This is the form you're
asking about, where entire words are repeated, often for emphasis or to
create a rhythmic effect.

> Example: "Would that the wood were seemingly seamless."
>
> This style can also be seen in phrases like "fair is fair," "man to man,"
> or "word for word."

**Compound Alliteration**: When consonant sounds are repeated in two or more
sets of compound words or multi-syllable words.

> "Wild and woolly, willfully in the woods"

**Paroemion**: Overuse of alliteration.

> Example: "We watched as westerly winds wickedly whipped the wilting
> weeping willows."

## Anaphora

Strategically repeating a word or phrase.

> "I had a dream that one day this nation will rise up.
> I had a dream that my four little children will one day live in a nation
> where they will not be judged by the color of their skin but by the
> content of their character.
> I had a dream today"

---

## Anadiplosis

Takes the last word and it becomes the first word.

> "We glory in tribulations also, knowing that tribulation worketh patience,
> and patience, experience, and experience, hope, and hope maketh man not
> ashamed."

## Epigram

A pithy saying or remark expressing an idea in a clever and amusing way.

> "You will never remember to reload, it's far better to master it and be
> so fast at reloading, it doesn't matter you forgot."

## Hypotactic / Hypotaxis

Very long, complicated sentences. It is the opposite of parataxis.

## Merism

Merism is a rhetorical device that involves referring to a whole by listing
its parts, often using contrasting elements to express a broader concept.
It's a way of implying totality or completeness by mentioning two or more
extremes or parts.

For example:

- "He searched high and low" (meaning he searched everywhere).
- "We need to protect both young and old" (meaning everyone).
- "You are either with me, or against me."

## Polysyndeton

Using lots of conjunctions is called **polysyndeton**. Total absence of
conjunctions is called **asyndeton**.

> Example: "I have time and time and time and she can move in the Next and
> Next and Next"

## Parataxis / Paratactic

Short sentences.

> "Writing should be a blend of things. Parataxis can be nice. It allows
> you to be understood. It explicitly follows conventions. It doesn't
> pollute sentences with complexity. It shuns adjectives. It shuns
> adjectives. It avoids conjunctions. It allows the reader to understand
> simple thoughts."

## Hypotaxis / Hypotactic (worked example)

> "The alternative, should you, or any writer of English, choose to employ
> it (and who is to stop you?) is, by use of subordinate clause upon
> subordinate clause, which itself may be subordinated to those clauses
> that have gone before or after, to construct a sentence of such
> labyrinthine grammatical complexity that, like Theseus before you when
> he searched the dark Minoan mazes for that monstrous monster, half bull
> and half man, or rather half woman for it had been conceived from, or
> in, Pasiphae, herself within a Daedalian contraption of perverted
> intention, you must unravel a ball of grammatical yarn lest you wander
> forever, amazed in the maze, searching through dark eternity for a full
> stop. That's hypotaxis, and it used to be everywhere."
>
> — Forsyth, Mark. *The Elements of Eloquence* (p. 75). Penguin Publishing
> Group. Kindle Edition.

## Rhetorical Questions

### Anacoenosis

Anacoenosis is a rhetorical device in which a speaker poses a question to
the audience or others in order to involve them in the discussion or to
establish a common ground. The question is typically one where the answer
is either obvious or aligns with the speaker's viewpoint, making it a
persuasive technique.

In short, it is a question that will always have an obvious answer, no
matter where it is asked, as long as it is relevant. At a company meeting,
CEO Stanley might say, "Who makes the best Cogs?" To which the gathered
employees will of course say, "We do!" Meanwhile, over at Spry Sprockets,
Inc, the CEO says, "Who makes the best Sprockets?!" To which the loyal
employees answer, "We do!"

This device serves several purposes:

- **Engagement**: By asking the audience a question, the speaker engages
  them directly, making them feel involved in the conversation.
- **Consensus Building**: The question often seeks agreement, subtly
  pushing the audience to align their thoughts with the speaker's argument.
- **Rhetorical Effect**: Anacoenosis can create a sense of unity or shared
  understanding between the speaker and the audience.

> Example: A politician might say, "Don't we all want a better future for
> our children?" Here, the question is not meant to be answered literally
> but to elicit a nod of agreement and create a sense of shared values or
> goals.

In essence, anacoenosis is a way to appeal to the audience's sense of
reason or emotion by presenting a question that guides them to agree with
the speaker's perspective.

### Anthypophora

A rhetorical device where the speaker poses a question and then immediately
answers it. It's used to anticipate objections and address them.

> Example from Shakespeare's *Julius Caesar*:
>
> "Did this in Caesar seem ambitious? When that the poor have cried, Caesar
> hath wept; Ambition should be made of sterner stuff. Yet Brutus says he
> was ambitious; And Brutus is an honourable man."
>
> Here, Antony raises the question of whether Caesar was ambitious and then
> immediately answers by citing evidence to the contrary.

### Antiphora

Often used interchangeably with anthypophora, this term also refers to the
technique of posing a question and then answering it oneself. It's a way to
control the narrative and guide the audience's thinking.

> Example from John Milton's *Areopagitica*:
>
> "What should ye do then? Should ye suppress all this flowery crop of
> knowledge?"
>
> Milton asks and answers his question to argue against censorship.
>
> "What is my path forward then? Should I just walk out and leave you?
> Should I perhaps go lay down in the grass, and wait for the roots and
> the ground to take me? Or should I stay here, and suffer your abuses?"

### Apocrisis

A rhetorical term where a question is posed and then answered, typically in
a formal or authoritative manner. It's used to establish a point or
argument by providing a clear answer to a question.

> Example from Joseph Conrad's *Heart of Darkness*:
>
> "What did he expect? I would not have believed him. I had rather my
> heart had been broken."
>
> Conrad poses and answers his rhetorical questions to reveal Marlow's
> internal conflict.

### Aporia

A rhetorical expression of doubt or perplexity, often used to express
uncertainty about how to proceed or to raise a question about a point. It
can be genuine or feigned to engage the audience or to highlight the
complexity of an issue.

> Example from Shakespeare's *Hamlet*:
>
> "To be, or not to be, that is the question: Whether 'tis nobler in the
> mind to suffer The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, Or to take
> arms against a sea of troubles And by opposing end them."
>
> Hamlet expresses doubt and perplexity about life and death, highlighting
> his indecision.

### Erotesis

A rhetorical question posed to make a point rather than to elicit an
answer. The question is asked to assert something or to emphasize a point,
expecting no answer because the answer is implied.

> Example from Charles Dickens's *A Tale of Two Cities*:
>
> "Is it possible! Is it possible that vile habit of mine should rob me of
> life in this manner? And that, too, at such a time?"
>
> Here, the rhetorical questions emphasize the character's disbelief and
> distress.

### Erotema

Another term for a rhetorical question, used for emphasis or persuasion.
Like erotesis, it implies that the answer is obvious or already known.

> Example from Mark Twain's *The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn*:
>
> "What's the use you learning to do right, when it's troublesome to do
> right and ain't no trouble to do wrong, and the wages is just the same?"
>
> Huck's rhetorical question emphasizes the futility he feels about moral
> choices.

### Epiplexis

A rhetorical question used to rebuke, shame, or criticize someone. It's
often accusatory or challenging, meant to provoke a reaction from the
listener.

> "What's the point?"
>
> "Why would you want that?"

### Epitemesis

Similar to epiplexis, however, the question is followed with an
accusation/judgment. A rhetorical question used to reprimand or rebuke. It
is similar to epiplexis but can also involve a sharp or critical statement
following the question.

> "What were you thinking when you ignored all the warnings? Do you have
> any idea how much damage you've caused?"
>
> "Why did you ignore my advice? Clearly, you've made a mess of things."

### Hypophora

A rhetorical strategy where the speaker raises a question and then
immediately answers it. This technique is often used to guide the audience
to the desired conclusion.

> "What have you done for me lately? NOTHING"

### Interrogatio

A rhetorical question asked for effect, where the answer is often obvious
and does not require a response. It's used to engage the audience or to
highlight a particular point.

### Percontatio

A rhetorical question used to probe deeply into a subject or issue, often
with the intention of revealing something hidden or unexpected. It's more
about inquiry and less about asserting an obvious point.

### Procatalepsis

Rhetorical device that involves anticipating and responding to an
opponent's objections or an audience's response before they can be voiced.
It's also known as prebuttal, prolepsis, anticipatio, or the figure of
presupposal.

### Pysma

A rhetorical technique involving a series of questions asked in rapid
succession. It's used to overwhelm the opponent or to create a sense of
urgency or pressure.

### Rogatio

Another term for asking a question, particularly in a formal or rhetorical
context. It can be used as a synonym for interrogatio or erotesis,
depending on the context.

### Ratiocinatio

A rhetorical device where a question is asked, and the answer involves
reasoning or argumentation. It's used to lead the audience through a
logical process, often to a specific conclusion.

### Subjectio

A rhetorical technique where the speaker asks a question and then answers
it with a pointed or assertive statement. It's used to emphasize the
speaker's point and to keep control of the argument.

## Tautology

A tautology is a rhetorical or logical term referring to a statement that
repeats the same idea in different words, often unnecessarily. It can also
mean a statement that is always true by its logical form, regardless of
the content.

In everyday language, tautology typically involves redundancy, where two
or more words convey the same meaning without adding new information.

For example:

- "Free gift" (a gift is inherently free, so adding 'free' is redundant).
- "I saw it with my own eyes" (since seeing implies using your eyes,
  'with my own eyes' is repetitive).

In logic, a tautology is a statement that is true in every possible
interpretation, such as:

- "It will either rain tomorrow or it won't."
- "A or not A" (where A is any proposition).

In both cases, tautologies are often seen as redundant or unnecessary,
though they can sometimes be used for emphasis.

> "If you begin to alphabetically sort something, the last word will always
> be the last word."
>
> In any list, the last word is always the last word until it is not, and
> the last word is always the last word, no matter where it is in the list.
> This describes two things that are different, and are both true.
